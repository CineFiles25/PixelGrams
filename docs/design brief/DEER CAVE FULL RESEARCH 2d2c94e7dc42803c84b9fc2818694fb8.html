BASIC INFORMATION

CategoryDetails
Name OriginNamed after the numerous depictions of deer on its walls, shown in various poses and hunting situations
Other Name • “Cave of Aeneas” (because it was believed Enea had landed here when he came to Italy)
• "Sistine Chapel of Prehistory" (due to the immense scale and importance of the paintings)
LocationPorto Badisco, Otranto (Lecce), Salento, Puglia, Italy - 15km south of Otranto
Geographic CoordinatesFacing the Strait of Otranto (Adriatic Sea)
SignificanceOne of the most extraordinary testimonies of Prehistoric art in Europe; richest collection of Neolithic rock paintings in quality and variety
Current StatusCLOSED TO PUBLIC since 1987 - accessible only to scientific community - due to the extreme fragility of the “faugnu” (humidity) microclimate (temperature and humidity) necessary for art preservation.

2nd corridorZone VLizard HallCircular room suitable for meetings. Lizard or gecko painted at the entrance.

2nd corridorZone VIIVestibuleAnte-chamber where an ancient guano deposit once stood. Precedes the Sanctuary.
2nd corridorZone VIIISanctuarySmall handprints on the ceiling (suggesting female or child hands), interpreted as a ritual gesture marking passage from childhood to adulthood. Controversial figure: Lady of the Serpents (female figure with breasts attached directly to the neck, triangular head with a seven-rayed crown, S-shaped body; two attributes under mirrored S-shaped legs). Linked by Maria Gimbutas to a Neolithic Mother Serpent Goddess as a symbol of cyclical time.

CULTURAL CONNECTIONS & EXCHANGE NETWORKS

The Deer Cave shares a rich mythological and religious system with other Euro-Mediterranean contexts across time.

"There are symbols that recur throughout the ancient world, such as the spiral (a symbol of life and regeneration attributed to the Mother Goddess), which is present in the pictograms at the Grotta dei Cervi and is the dominant decorative motif on the ceramics found there.. But most of the symbols are a mystery. In reality, rather than looking at individual symbols, in my opinion we should consider the work as a whole: along the route, there is a narrative unfolding, a history shared and known to those who frequented the cave. The cave must have been the equivalent of what we consider a sanctuary or a place of worship. It certainly houses the richest collection of symbols dating back to the Neolithic—a true ideological manifesto of prehistory—in the entire Western world.”
Elettra Ingravallo, professor of Palaeontology at the University of Salento.
