Grotta dei Cervi — PROJECT CONCEPT

Grotta dei Cervi VR is a role-based, archaeologically accurate, multi-level virtual descent into one of Europe's most mysterious prehistoric caves — where every visitor becomes part of the ritual, and leaves their own mark on the wall.

💡 The Core Idea

A multi-character VR experience hosted in the Otranto Castle (Castello Aragonese di Otranto), where visitors physically descend into darkness just as the Neolithic people who inhabited it did thousands of years ago. The experience is not a passive museum visit — each visitor inhabits a role inside the cave and discovers its art through that lens.

Education happens through immersion, not instruction.

The guiding metaphor: the user journey IS the descent into darkness.

🏛️ The Setting

VR headsets in a dim, atmospherically lit room inside the Otranto Castle — the ideal host since it already holds a permanent exhibition on the Deer Cave and is geographically the closest institution to Porto Badisco.

Before entering the VR, visitors encounter a physical preamble station: infographics and images explaining why a) the cave is inaccessible, b) what they are about to see, c) accessibility warnings (VR side effects, colorblind and deaf-friendly design), and a d) key invitation — if you are in a group, choose different characters so you can compare your experiences afterward.

Staff greet visitors, explain the dynamics, help fit the headsets, and then step back.

From this point on, the visitor is alone in the cave.

🚶🏻 The Experience Structure

The Opening Scene

The visitor materialises at the West entrance of the cave at sunset in the 2020s. The Apulian sea glitters behind them, the last light of a summer evening washing over the Salento landscape. It is beautiful — and brief. There is no time to linger outside. Something draws you in.

A single question appears:

Who are you?

  1. 🐾 Shaman — the main, most complete storyline (can be either man or woman)
  1. ⚔️ Warrior — entering to seek protection or power
  1. 👁️ Devotee I — seeking communication with ancestors and initiation rituals
  1. 🙏 Devotee II — bringing an offering, asking something of the deity
  1. 💃 Dancer — part of the ritual, keeper of movement and trance

We are now a person of the Neolithic, we’re back in time.

Inside the Cave

The educational content — descriptions of paintings, historical context, significance of symbols — is the same for all characters. What changes is the experiences.

The Shaman performs the ritual and interprets the phosphenes as visions.
The Devotee II must choose the right votive offering for their request, learning about the artifacts through that act of choice.
The Dancer experiences the cave through movement and sound.

Each role makes the same knowledge feel personal.

As visitors move deeper into the cave, the experience follows the real spatial logic of the site — Zone by Zone, from the Red Room outward — mirroring the sacred path the Neolithic painters themselves walked.

Key moments include:

  • Encountering a 3D model of Professor Valzano (the project's academic anchor) who contextualises discoveries as you go;
  • Recognising a painting and seeing it linked to identical symbols found in caves worldwide — from California to Colombia — reinforcing the universal nature of phosphenic imagery
  • A moment of genuine darkness and silence deep in the cave, where the visitor understands what the shamans felt

The Ending: Your Mark on the World

Transition between the VR Experience and the exit from the Aragonese Castle. An interactive LED Display (either on the wall or on the ground).

Once out of the cave, other caves around the world with similar pictograms are shown, to highlight the common language shared by communities back then.

The final scene poses a single, deceptively simple question:

If you were a Neolithic painter, what would you draw?

The visitor is given a specific contemporary concept (object — assigned by the experience, not chosen by them. Something universal: a phone, a journey, a family, a celebration, death, love, time. They are asked to draw it abstractly, with no letters or realistic representation. Just marks, shapes, lines — the way the Badisco painters did.

The real revelation comes after: their drawing is placed alongside every other visitor's drawing of the same concept, gathered from all over the world and over time. The question the project asks — and answers — is:

Despite our differences, do we still picture things the sam e way?
Despite our borders, are we still part of one, big community?

If the drawings converge — if a spiral still means something spinning, if concentric circles still suggest depth or importance, if a zigzag still means water or danger — then the cave paintings stop being ancient and alien. They become a mirror. The phosphenes were never just Neolithic. They were always ours.

This transforms the mini-game from a fun activity into the intellectual and emotional thesis of the entire project.

Outside of the VR: Handprints’ Interactive Display

Upon exiting the VR experience, the visitor finds themselves in front of a touch-reactive LED screen. All the handprints left by those who lived the experience before them are visible on the screen — luminous silhouettes on a dark background, arranged randomly, like the handprints on the ceiling of the Sanctuary.

The visitor places their own hand on the screen: the print is added to the others with a brief light effect and remains.

This gesture directly mirrors the handprints in the Sanctuary of the Grotta dei Cervi — the most immediate and universal symbol of presence: "I was here." The accumulation of prints over time makes the belonging to a single humanity visible, regardless of the chosen character, the level, or the story experienced.

🎨 Visual Identity & Style

The entire visual language of the project — VR interface, website, printed materials — draws directly from the cave's own artistic vocabulary:

  • Colours: red and black (the cave's pigments), on a warm beige or off-white ground
    • Red Ochre (iron oxide / hematite)
      • HEX: #8B3A2A
      • RGB: rgb(139, 58, 42)
    • Black Guano (bat guano, manganese-based black)
      • HEX: #1C1A17
      • RGB: rgb(28, 26, 23)
    • warm limestone/beige as a neutral background:
      • HEX: #C9B99A
      • RGB: rgb(201, 185, 154)
  • Shapes: phosphenic forms — spirals, concentric circles, labyrinths, grids, dots — used as decorative and navigational elements
  • Motion: the same dashes and chevrons the shamans used to indicate movement animate transitions and loading screens
  • Feeling: the design should feel trance-adjacent — rhythmic, repetitive, hypnotic — without being inaccessible

The website mirrors the exhibition. As Professor Pescarini noted, the website is the mirror of the museum — so the same symbolic vocabulary, the same darkness and warmth, the same sense of descending into something ancient.

♿ Accessibility & Accuracy

  • Full colorblind-friendly palette and interface options
  • Subtitles and visual cues for deaf visitors throughout
  • No heavy physical movement required — designed to work seated if needed
  • VR side-effect warnings clearly communicated before the experience begins
  • All content historically and archaeologically grounded, with Professor Valzano's 3D presence as the guarantee of academic rigour
