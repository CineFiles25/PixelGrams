3. CONCEPT

Here you specify:

  • Problem/s you are facing with your project, try to be specific (i.e. attracting an audience that usually is not interested to museums):
    • The Grotta dei Cervi has been inaccessible to the public since 1987, making it impossible to visit, photograph, or experience directly — even for most researchers
    • The cave is largely unknown outside of academic and local circles, despite being one of Europe's richest Neolithic painted sites
    • Attracting an audience that is not typically interested in prehistory or museums, and making the experience meaningful across very different visitor profiles (local elderly, tourists, international researchers) → Audience disengagement: the cave's scholarly profile is strong but its public profile is weak. Tourists in Salento are primarily drawn by the coastline; cultural heritage competes for attention against leisure. The challenge is not just accessibility but motivation — making someone who wasn't planning a museum visit feel that this experience is unmissable.
    • The experience risks feeling like a passive museum visit rather than something genuinely immersive and memorable; the design must actively counter this expectation
    • VR as a medium presents accessibility barriers: physical discomfort, low digital literacy in older or local audiences, and the need for trained staff on site
  • How your project will face the problem/s:
    • By hosting the experience at the Castello Aragonese di Otranto — already a heritage destination — we meet the audience where they already are, lowering the barrier to entry
    • An intrecative experiience branching narrative with five character roles (Shaman, Warrior, Devotee I, Devotee II, Dancer) allows each visitor to engage at their own pace and level of interest and learning speed;
    • The choice architecture also addresses audience disengagement: the branching structure creates replayability and social motivation, particularly for groups, who are encouraged by staff to choose different characters and compare experiences afterward, creating social motivation through fun activites;
    • The closing pictogram activity — asking visitors to draw a contemporary concept abstractly — bridges the ancient and the contemporary, making the experience personally relevant regardless of background or education level
    • Staff presence at the installation guides first-time VR users and contextualizes the experience before and after
  • Museological Approach:
    • The project focuses primarily on the audience: rather than starting from the collection and asking how to display it, every design decision begins with the visitor, the capabilities and their feelings. The cave's pictograms, zones, and artifacts are all present, but they are always encountered through the lens of a specific visitor role, motivation, and emotional state.
    • This approach is grounded in an interpretive museology framework, guided by Freeman Tilden's six principles of interpretation (Interpreting Our Heritage, 1957). Tilden held that any interpretation that does not relate what is being displayed to something within the personality or experience of the visitor will be sterile: a principle that drives every design choice in this project. Successful interpretation requires not only good information but a robust mix of media and sensory experiences designed to stimulate curiosity, challenge intellect, and appeal to visitors' emotions. Accordingly, DigHerit is built around immersion and active participation rather than passive information delivery. The visitor does not read about the cave — they descend into it, inhabit a role within it, and leave their own mark on it.
    • Inspirations (based ourselves on Jakobs’Law: familiriaty does help with the users,so we based our experiences on prexisting ones):
      • the VR experience at the Rafa Nadal Academy Museum in Manacor, Mallorca), and how VR can transform a museum visit from passive observation into active participation, using digital technologies and virtual reality to experience sports that most users will never experience in their life (e.g. extreme sports) and learn the values of the sport. Experienced by Claudia personally;
      • DDR Museum in Berlin, Germany: many experiences that blend analogic objects from the time and digital technologies (like interactive displays) to explain their history, description and purpose. Experienced by Laura personally.

(d) The story (you can include an interactive narrative using Twine)

I. The Opening: The Liminal Threshold
The journey begins at the West entrance of the cave during a Salento sunset. The visitor is standing in a "360° Panorama" where the Apulian sea glitters behind them in the last light of a summer evening. This beauty is brief; the narrative creates a sense of urgency, drawing the visitor away from the modern landscape and into the dark mouth of the cave.

At this threshold, a single question defines the journey: "Who are you?". The visitor must select one of five Neolithic identities, each offering a unique lens through which to experience the cave:

• 🐾 Shaman: The most complete narrative path.
• ⚔️ Warrior: Seeking protection or power.
• 👁️ Devotee I: Seeking initiation and communication with ancestors.
• 🙏 Devotee II: Bringing a votive offering to a deity.
• 💃 Dancer: A keeper of movement and trance.

II. Inside the Cave: Personalizing Universal Knowledge
While the educational core—the history of the pictograms and the significance of the 12 Zones—remains consistent, the reason for encountering it changes based on the chosen role. The experience follows the real spatial logic of the site, moving from the Red Room outward, mirroring the sacred path of the original Neolithic painters.

• The Ritual of Learning: The Shaman interprets paintings as phosphenic visions, while the Devotee II learns about Neolithic artifacts by choosing the correct votive offering (Ceramic, Obsidian, or Pintaderas) for their petition.
• Academic Guidance: Throughout the descent, a 3D model of Professor Valzano serves as the academic anchor, providing archaeological context for the discoveries.

• The Sensory Core: A pivotal moment occurs deep in the cave where the visitor experiences absolute darkness and silence, recreating the sensory deprivation that ancient shamans used to trigger phosphenic imagery.

III. The Finale: Intertextuality and the Sanctuary
At the heart of the cave, the narrative expands from a local story to a global one. The visitor sees paintings linked to identical symbols found in caves from California to Colombia, reinforcing the project's thesis: that this visual language is a universal human trait.

IV. The Ending: Your Mark on the World
As the visitor prepares to exit the VR, the experience poses a final challenge: "If you were a Neolithic painter, what would you draw?".

• The Concept Drawing: The system assigns a universal contemporary concept—such as Family, Death, or A Phone—and asks the visitor to draw it abstractly using shapes and lines, just as the Badisco painters did.
• The Revelation: The user’s drawing is placed in a "Global Archive" alongside those of other visitors. This comparison answers the project's emotional thesis: if our modern shapes converge with ancient ones, the pictograms are no longer alien—they are a mirror of our shared humanity.

V. The Physical Outro: The Collective Handprint
The experience concludes as the visitor removes the headset and transitions to a physical touch-reactive LED screen in the Otranto Castle room.

• A Living Archive: The screen displays a luminous cloud of handprints left by previous visitors, arranged randomly like the ceiling of the Sanctuary.

• The Act of Presence: By placing their own hand on the screen, the visitor’s print is added to the local network instantly. This final gesture bridges the gap between the virtual Neolithic past and the physical present, making the belonging to a single, continuous humanity visible and permanent.

6. DISRUPTION

Here you can explain threats and potential issues with your project and how you would face it

  • High production and installation costs
    A full VR installation with four headsets, custom narrative content, and a professional Figma/Twine prototype requires significant investment. However, the project makes a strong case for public and private funding: it generates tourism, demonstrates regional innovation, promotes ethical cultural heritage exploration, and positions Puglia as a leader in digital heritage practice. Partnerships with the Castello Aragonese, the Municipality of Otranto, regional cultural funds (e.g. Puglia Region cultural grants, MiC), and European heritage programmes (e.g. Creative Europe) could absorb much of the cost.
  • Permission and copyright issues with Virginia Valzano's 3D assets
    The academic foundation of this project is deeply rooted in Valzano's pioneering 3D reconstructions. To maximize the impact of this research, it is crucial that permissions are granted and collaborative avenues remain open. When institutions unite to enhance and celebrate a historical landmark, traditional restrictions should give way to shared progress, ultimately benefiting the global academic community and showcasing the power of collaborative preservation.
  • VR-induced discomfort and health risks
    A significant portion of visitors — particularly older adults, children, and people prone to motion sickness — may experience nausea, disorientation, or anxiety. This is mitigated by: clear VR side-effect warnings at the preamble station, a seated experience option, short session duration (estimated 20–25 minutes), trained staff monitoring visitors throughout, and an immediate exit option at any point in the experience.
  • Low digital literacy among target visitors
    Visitors like Annunziata — older, locally rooted, unfamiliar with VR — may feel intimidated by the technology. This is addressed by the staff onboarding protocol, the physical preamble station which builds familiarity before the headset is introduced, and the simplicity of the controller interaction design (single button presses, no complex gestures).
  • Technical failure during the experience
    Hardware malfunction, software crashes, or connectivity issues could interrupt or ruin a visitor's experience. Mitigation strategies include: regular technical maintenance checks before opening hours, backup headsets on standby, staff trained to troubleshoot basic issues, and a printed fallback version of key content at the preamble station so the visit retains educational value even without VR.
  • Inaccessibility for visitors with severe physical disabilities
    While the experience is designed to be usable seated and includes colorblind-safe design and subtitles, visitors with severe motor impairments may still find VR controllers difficult to use. Future development should include eye-tracking navigation as an alternative input method, which is already available on several current VR headsets.

7. UX: TWINE

The Twine scenario of the project can be found at the following link on our website: https://cinefiles25.github.io/PixelGrams/twine/index.html

Based on the storyboard developed in the previous stage, my role was to translate the narrative into an interactive experience.

I focused on the visual and interaction design of the Twine prototype, including page structure, navigation, text hierarchy, images, and visual effects. The goal was not only to communicate the story, but also to support the atmosphere of the cave experience.

Since visitors cannot access the real cave, I used a minimal visual language inspired by the cave environment, including dark backgrounds, ochre-red highlights, subtle glow effects, and carefully paced text layouts. These elements were used to create a sense of mystery, discovery, and ritual while guiding users through the narrative.

The final structure was designed to balance storytelling, interaction, and atmosphere while following the flow of the storyboard.

PROTOTYPE / DESIGN

Hi-Fi Figma Prototype — DigHerit VR Experience

We built a complete hi-fi prototype in Figma covering the full user journey from physical arrival to post-experience reflection.

Screen flow

The journey begins at the pre-VR physical panel (E1), which introduces the experience before the headset is put on. From there, the cave entrance screen (V0) presents a panoramic background with a movement prompt. Users then reach the character selection screen (V1), where they choose one of five roles.

All characters pass through the Red Hall (V2), designed as the shared common space, populated with actual pictogram imagery and atmospheric lighting. At this branching point, five parallel paths diverge — Shaman, Warrior, Devotee I, Devotee II, and Dancer — each with its own narrative screen, a role-specific interaction (ritual choice, offering selection, ancestor figure recognition, or rhythm circle), and a continue button.

All paths then converge in the Sanctuary (V4), centered on the handprint moment. The experience continues with the global phosphenic map screen (V5), which shows cave sites across five continents, and concludes with the physical LED handprint display (P1) outside the headset.

After exiting the cave, the V6 panel presents a placeholder starry-sky scene — in the final version, this would be replaced by a nighttime panoramic view of Porto Badisco photographed by us. Users see cave imagery from around the world and receive the "Draw Your Picture" prompt, which builds curiosity and anticipation for V7. In the V7 panel, users draw either a contemporary concept (for example, a phone, burnout, or a milkshake) or a Neolithic-era concept (for example, love or hunting) rendered in today's visual language. In the real VR implementation, this would be done with controllers using ray casting. The final step compares each user's drawing with those of previous visitors, creating a reflective takeaway — consistent with strong museum experience design, where the visit begins before the headset and continues after it.

Alongside the VR screens, we designed a set of pictogram ID cards (P1–P4) in a consistent format: image column, title, dividing line, and descriptive text. These serve as the interpretive layer of the exhibition.

Visual and aesthetic design

Starting from the lo-fi prototype's established layout and color palette, we built an interactive version with images, screens, and buttons. To simulate a VR feeling inside Figma, panel images can be dragged around. To suggest cave humidity, most panels are set to 60% opacity.

For visuals, we used cave photos where available, placeholders from other caves, and stock images sourced from Unsplash via a Figma plugin. For the 360 view, we linked a proprietary Porto Badisco panorama from 360Cities as a stand-in for the final version: a sunset panoramic view of Porto Badisco photographed by us and stitched in PTGui.

The color system is drawn from the cave itself as it would appear when lit: light beige, black guano, and red ochre. The typeface is IM Fell English, a Google Font whose hand-drawn quality recalls pictographic writing.

No music was added because the free version of Figma does not support the required audio setup. In the final implementation, the idea would be to use subtle cave drips or soft ambient sound — nothing too overwhelming, since immersive experiences can already be intense for more sensitive users.

UX/UI principles

The interface design follows several core principles:

  • Fitt's Law: all interactive elements are large enough to click comfortably.
  • Gestalt principles: visual grouping improves readability and spatial orientation.
  • Hick's Law: choices are limited and clearly presented, reducing cognitive load.
  • Miller's Law: information is delivered in manageable chunks as a direct consequence of applying Hick's Law.
  • Tidwell's preattentive variables: visual hierarchy ensures key elements are immediately distinguishable.

Accessibility and tools

Figma plugins were used to check accessibility, color contrast, and related UI quality criteria. A settings menu allows users to adjust sound, color theme, and font size.

Green-dot annotations are placed throughout the prototype to flag features that could not be fully implemented due to budget, resource, or rights constraints.

Content and narrative design

For the Red Hall, reusable components were built for each character path so that the hall remains structurally consistent across all branches, sharing the same buttons and assets. Some pictogram descriptions are intentionally incomplete, since reliable data on individual symbols is limited; this content would be expanded by domain experts. The current Red Hall image is not from that exact room but was selected as the closest available representative image.

The narrative assumes users have first encountered the artifacts in the Castle exhibition. If they have not, the experience may prompt them to go back and look more carefully afterward — which is itself a valid outcome.

No character images are used anywhere in the prototype, by design. The goal is deeper immersion: users become the character and imagine their own appearance and identity freely.

Character paths were designed with likely user motivations in mind. The Shaman and Devotee paths emphasize ritual decisions and reflection; the Devotee paths may appeal more to users with a spiritual or religious inclination; the Dancer path focuses on embodied movement rather than explicit choices, and may attract more physically active users. No path leads to a wrong outcome. Users are sometimes asked to reflect on whether a choice felt right, but all routes remain valid.

The storylines are interconnected: the devotee figure in the Shaman path is one of the Devotees, the other Devotee is the person who clashes with the Guardian, and the Dancer witnesses these ritual dynamics from a different vantage point. This structure opens the door to future multiplayer development.

In the Sanctuary, the sequence begins in darkness with phosphenic imagery, evoking the visual phenomena associated with cave ritual contexts. A "Look Up" prompt gives users a clear action, with further instructions to follow so as not to overload them. The intended design would delay any UI overlays — title, info panel, continue button — for at least ten seconds to strengthen immersion, but this timing behavior could not be reproduced in Figma Free.

Goals

Both main project goals are addressed: presenting the Grotta dei Cervi and its pictograms in an immersive format, and prompting visitors to reflect on community and universal visual language — asking, ultimately, whether we still see the world the same way.
